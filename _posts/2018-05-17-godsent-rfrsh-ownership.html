On May 13th, Richard Lewis released a video on Valve's new Conflict of Interest rule which is being implemented for the upcoming FaceIt London Major and the Qualifiers. The rule is very similar to the WESA Multi-team Ownership rule that was announced in March of 2017.

The rule as stated under the FACEIT Major Open Qualifier Rules, Section 6.7 is:

{% include quote.html text="Teams and players should not have any financial interest in the success of any team that they are competing against. To participate in the 2018 Fall Major, players and teams are required to affirm that they have no business entanglement (including, but not limited to, shared managed, shared ownership of entities, licensing, and loans) with any other participating team or its players. If teams or players have an agreement or business arrangement that may be of concern, then please reach out to the tournament officials for further discussion."%}

As Richard Lewis pointed out, the two largest organizations that this will affect are RFRSH and ESForce. Both own multiple teams in the Counter-Strike Professional scene.

However, at the 7:30 mark of his video, Richard Lewis made the claim that RFRSH Entertainment had not sold GODSENT, implying that they own the team in some manner.

In order to uncover whether or not this was true, Esports Kingdom reached out to RFRSH, and we received prompt replies from the company. According to Steen Laursen, the VP of Communications and Brand;

{% include quote.html text="We do not have ownership in GODSENT, which has been communicated on several occasions and which is also clearly indicated on our digital platforms, where we do not mention GODSENT under our properties."%}
